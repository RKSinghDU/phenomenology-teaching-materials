# Assessment 2 · Phenomenological Research Protocol

**Weighting:** 60% | **Word Limit:** 4,600 words (excluding references and interview extract)

---

## Task

Design a complete phenomenological research protocol for an original doctoral-level study in the domain of trauma, resilience, or adversity. The protocol has six required sections.

---

## Required Sections

### Section 1 — Title and Abstract (300 words)

Provide:
- A full title that names the phenomenon, the population, and (optionally) the methodological approach.
- An abstract of no more than 300 words that specifies:
  - The phenomenon under investigation
  - The population from which participants will be drawn
  - The level of eidetic generality being sought (very general, mid-level, highly specific/contextual)
  - The phenomenological method to be used
  - The anticipated contribution of the study

**Guidance:** The title and abstract should make it immediately clear that this is a phenomenological study, not a general qualitative or mixed-methods study. The phenomenon should be precisely named — not 'resilience' but 'the experience of recovering a sense of future following prolonged bereavement.'

---

### Section 2 — Philosophical and Theoretical Framework (800 words)

Situate your study within the phenomenological tradition by:
- Identifying which phenomenological thinkers are most relevant to your phenomenon and explaining why.
- Describing the key philosophical concepts your study will draw on (e.g., embodiment, temporality, intersubjectivity) and showing how they illuminate the phenomenon.
- Distinguishing your approach from related but different traditions (e.g., IPA, grounded theory, narrative inquiry).
- Identifying the level of eidetic generality you are working at and justifying this choice.

**Guidance:** This section should demonstrate that your philosophical choices are principled, not arbitrary. If you are drawing on Merleau-Ponty, explain specifically which aspects of his phenomenology of embodiment your study will use, and why. Avoid generic overviews of 'phenomenology in general.'

---

### Section 3 — Methodology (1,000 words)

Describe and justify your methodological choices across the following elements:

**Method choice:** Which specific phenomenological method will you use — Giorgi's descriptive method, IPA (Smith et al.), van Manen's hermeneutic phenomenology, Moustakas's transcendental phenomenology, or another? Justify your choice with reference to the nature of your phenomenon and research question.

**Participant selection:** What are your inclusion/exclusion criteria? How will you recruit? How many participants do you anticipate, and how will you determine sufficiency?

**Data collection:** What is your interview design? Provide your opening question and at least two phenomenologically sensitive probe questions. Justify the interview format (unstructured, semi-structured) with reference to your phenomenon.

**Analysis procedure:** Describe your analysis process step by step, using the method you have chosen. Be specific — do not describe 'thematic analysis' as if it were equivalent to phenomenological analysis.

**Representation:** How will you represent your findings? What will a reader see — individual structural descriptions, a general structural description, both?

---

### Section 4 — Ethical Framework (600 words)

Identify and substantively address at least **four** ethical challenges specific to your study. At least two must be specifically phenomenological rather than generic qualitative research ethics concerns.

For each challenge, describe:
- The nature of the ethical issue
- Why it is specifically significant for this study
- Your proposed response

**Examples of specifically phenomenological ethical challenges:**
- The risk of re-traumatisation through deep phenomenological interviewing
- The researcher's own embodied response to traumatic testimony (secondary traumatisation)
- The tension between respecting participant autonomy and researcher responsibility in managing distressing disclosure
- The limits of researcher competence when participants enter acute distress
- The Levinasian obligation of witness and what it demands beyond standard ethics codes

---

### Section 5 — Pilot Interview Extract and Analysis (1,500 words)

This section has two parts:

**Part A — Interview Extract (minimum 500 words, not counted in word limit)**

Provide a verbatim extract from a pilot interview you have conducted, OR a published phenomenological interview excerpt used with appropriate attribution. The extract should be from an interview exploring the phenomenon (or a closely related phenomenon) your study addresses.

**Part B — Preliminary Analysis (1,000 words, counted in word limit)**

Demonstrate the first two steps of your chosen analysis procedure applied to the extract:

*If using Giorgi's method:*
1. Reading for overall sense
2. Delineation of meaning units (mark the extract to show your meaning unit boundaries and explain your rationale for three of them)
3. Preliminary transformation of three meaning units into psychological language

*If using IPA:*
1. Close reading and initial noting (annotate the extract with experiential comments, linguistic comments, and conceptual comments for a 150-word section)
2. Emergent themes for that section (list and briefly explain three themes)

*If using another method:* Apply the first two analytic steps of your chosen method and explain each step clearly.

**Guidance:** This section should demonstrate that you can actually apply the method — not just describe it. Markers are looking for evidence that you understand the difference between meaning units and themes, between description and interpretation, between the participant's language and psychological language.

---

### Section 6 — Reflexive Memo (400 words)

Write a genuine reflexive memo documenting:
- Your prior assumptions about the phenomenon
- The theoretical frameworks that shape how you think about it
- Any personal experience (direct or professional) that connects you to the topic
- Specific things you will need to actively bracket during analysis — stated as explicit commitments: *"I will need to bracket my assumption that..."*

**Guidance:** This memo should be candid, specific, and analytical — not a performative gesture toward reflexivity. Vague statements like "I may have biases" are insufficient. Name the specific assumptions, name the specific theoretical commitments, and describe a specific bracketing practice.

## Assessment Criteria

| Criterion | Description | Weighting |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Methodological rigour** | Method is chosen, described, and justified with sophistication; analysis procedure is applied correctly and specifically | 30% |
| **Philosophical grounding** | Philosophical choices are principled and precise; correct use of primary phenomenological concepts | 25% |
| **Ethical sensitivity** | Genuinely phenomenological ethical challenges identified and addressed substantively | 15% |
| **Quality of pilot analysis** | Meaning units / initial noting / first analytic step applied correctly; phenomenological language used accurately | 15% |
| **Reflexive awareness** | Bracketing memo is specific, candid, and analytical | 10% |
| **Scholarly presentation** | APA 7th edition referencing; clear structure; fluent academic writing | 5% |

---

## Common Errors to Avoid

- **Describing the method without applying it.** Section 5 requires you to actually analyse extract — not describe what Giorgi says to do.
- **Treating IPA as phenomenological analysis.** IPA is interpretive, not purely descriptive; if you use IPA, be clear about this distinction.
- **Generic ethics.** Stating that you will 'maintain confidentiality' and 'obtain informed consent' does not fulfil Section 4. These are baseline requirements, not specifically phenomenological ethical challenges.
- **Imprecise phenomenon naming.** 'Resilience' is a concept, not a phenomenon. The phenomenon is what people *experience* — name it at the level of lived experience.
- **Performative reflexivity.** Acknowledging that you 'might have biases' without naming them is not a reflexive memo. Name your assumptions explicitly.
