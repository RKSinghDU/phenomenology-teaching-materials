# Assessment 1 · Critical Conceptual Essay

**Weighting:** 40% &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Word Limit:** 4,000 words (excluding references)

---

## Task

Write a 4,000-word critical essay addressing **one** of the following three questions.

---

### Essay Option A

> *"The phenomenological account of trauma as lifeworld disruption is more philosophically rigorous but clinically less useful than the cognitive-behavioural account."*

Critically evaluate this claim. Your essay should:
- Accurately characterise both the phenomenological account (drawing on at least three thinkers) and the cognitive-behavioural account (e.g., Ehlers & Clark's cognitive model of PTSD).
- Identify where the two accounts converge and where they genuinely diverge.
- Critically assess the claim that philosophical rigour and clinical utility are in tension.
- Reach and defend a clear evaluative position.

---

### Essay Option B

> To what extent does Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of the lived body provide a theoretically adequate framework for understanding the somatic dimensions of traumatic experience? What are its limits?

Your essay should:
- Give a precise account of Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of embodiment, drawing on primary sources.
- Apply this framework to at least three somatic dimensions of trauma.
- Identify at least two significant limitations.
- Assess whether these limitations are fatal to the framework or can be addressed.

---

### Essay Option C

> *"Resilience is a normative concept that pathologises those who do not recover."*

Critically evaluate this claim from a phenomenological perspective. Your essay should:
- Clarify what is meant by calling resilience a 'normative concept.'
- Distinguish the everyday/policy use of 'resilience' from the phenomenological reconceptualisation in the chapter.
- Assess whether the phenomenological account addresses or fails to address the critique.
- Draw on both empirical literature (e.g., PTG research) and phenomenological scholarship.

---

## Assessment Criteria

| Criterion | Description | Weighting |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Philosophical precision** | Concepts used with accuracy; primary sources engaged directly | 30% |
| **Critical depth** | Arguments assessed, not merely summarised; evaluative position defended | 25% |
| **Integration of sources** | Primary texts, secondary scholarship, and empirical literature synthesised coherently | 20% |
| **Clarity of argument** | Clear thesis, logical structure, coherent progression | 15% |
| **Scholarly presentation** | Correct referencing; philosophical conventions; fluent academic writing | 10% |

---

## Guidance Notes

- Essays that merely summarise phenomenological positions without critical engagement will not achieve high marks.
- Engagement with **primary phenomenological texts** is required — secondary accounts alone are insufficient.
- A clear evaluative position defended with reasons and evidence is expected.
- Word limit is strict: 4,000 words excluding references. No 10% allowance.
