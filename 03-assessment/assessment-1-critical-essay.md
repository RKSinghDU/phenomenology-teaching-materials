# Assessment 1 · Critical Conceptual Essay

**Weighting:** 40% | **Word Limit:** 4,000 words (excluding references)

---

## Task

Write a 4,000-word critical essay addressing **one** of the following questions:

---

### Essay Option A

> *"The phenomenological account of trauma as lifeworld disruption is more philosophically rigorous but clinically less useful than the cognitive-behavioural account."*

Critically evaluate this claim. Your essay should:
- Accurately characterise both the phenomenological account (drawing on at least three thinkers from the tradition) and the cognitive-behavioural account (e.g., Ehlers & Clark's cognitive model of PTSD).
- Identify where the two accounts converge and where they genuinely diverge.
- Critically assess the claim that philosophical rigour and clinical utility are in tension — can this tension be resolved, or is it inherent?
- Reach and defend a clear evaluative position.

---

### Essay Option B

> To what extent does Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of the lived body provide a theoretically adequate framework for understanding the somatic dimensions of traumatic experience? What are the limits of this framework?

Your essay should:
- Give a precise and substantive account of Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of embodiment, drawing on primary sources.
- Apply this framework systematically to at least three somatic dimensions of trauma (e.g., hyperarousal, somatic flashbacks, bodily shame, dissociation).
- Identify at least two significant limitations — these may be internal to the phenomenological framework, or arise from empirical findings that the framework cannot easily accommodate.
- Assess whether these limitations are fatal to the framework or can be addressed through development or supplementation.

---

### Essay Option C

> *"Resilience is a normative concept that pathologises those who do not recover."*

Critically evaluate this claim from a phenomenological perspective. Your essay should:
- Clarify what is meant by calling resilience a 'normative concept' and what the pathologisation concern amounts to.
- Distinguish between the everyday/policy use of 'resilience' and the phenomenological reconceptualisation developed in the chapter.
- Assess whether the phenomenological account — particularly its emphasis on transformation rather than return to baseline — addresses or fails to address the critique.
- Draw on relevant empirical literature (e.g., post-traumatic growth research) and phenomenological scholarship in your argument.

## Assessment Criteria

| Criterion | Description | Weighting |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Philosophical precision** | Concepts are used with accuracy and doctoral-level sophistication; primary sources engaged directly | 30% |
| **Critical depth** | Arguments are assessed, not merely summarised; evaluative position is defended, not merely stated | 25% |
| **Integration of sources** | Primary phenomenological texts, secondary scholarship, and relevant empirical literature are synthesised coherently | 20% |
| **Clarity of argument** | Essay has a clear thesis, logical structure, and coherent progression of argument | 15% |
| **Scholarly presentation** | Appropriate referencing; correct use of philosophical conventions; fluent academic writing | 10% |

---

## Guidance Notes

- Essays that merely summarise phenomenological positions without critical engagement will not achieve high marks.
- You are expected to engage with **primary phenomenological texts** (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Levinas, Giorgi) directly — secondary accounts alone are insufficient.
- A clear evaluative position defended with reasons and evidence is expected. Essays that merely present 'on the one hand... on the other hand' without reaching a conclusion will be marked down.
- Word limit is strict: 4,000 words excluding references. There is no 10% allowance.
