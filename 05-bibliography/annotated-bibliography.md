# Annotated Bibliography

Texts organised by thematic cluster. Doctoral scholars are expected to engage with primary texts in their full form rather than through secondary summaries.

---

## I. Philosophical Foundations

### Husserl, E. (1913/1982). *Ideas: General Introduction to Pure Phenomenology* (F. Kersten, Trans.). Nijhoff.

The foundational text of phenomenology. Parts I and II are essential for understanding intentionality, the epoché, and eidetic reduction. Part III on the 'region of pure consciousness' is more technical and can be read selectively. Difficult but irreplaceable — the concepts that underlie all phenomenological psychology originate here.

**Priority sections for this course:** §§1–17 (introduction and natural attitude), §§27–62 (epoché and reduction), §§86–96 (intentionality and noema/noesis).

---

### Heidegger, M. (1927/1962). *Being and Time* (J. Macquarrie & E. Robinson, Trans.). Harper & Row.

The existential analytic that transformed phenomenology into a philosophy of human existence. Division One (§§12–42) on being-in-the-world, spatiality, and mood is directly applicable to trauma phenomenology. Division Two on temporality and authenticity is important for understanding how trauma disrupts the projective structure of Dasein.

**Recommended companion:** Dreyfus, H. L. (1991). *Being-in-the-World: A Commentary on Heidegger's Being and Time, Division I.* MIT Press. More accessible than Heidegger and essential for clinically-oriented readers.

---

### Merleau-Ponty, M. (1945/2012). *Phenomenology of Perception* (D. Landes, Trans.). Routledge.

Essential for embodiment. Part I (The Body) and Part II (The World as Perceived) are most directly relevant. The Landes translation (2012) is more accurate and readable than the older Smith translation and should be preferred. The chapter on the body's motor intentionality is key for understanding how trauma disrupts pre-reflective bodily skill and orientation.

**Priority sections:** Introduction (critique of empiricism and intellectualism), Part I Chapters 1–4, Part II Chapter 1.

---

### Levinas, E. (1969). *Totality and Infinity: An Essay on Exteriority* (A. Lingis, Trans.). Duquesne University Press.

Levinas's major work on the ethics of the Other. Section II on 'Interiority and Economy' and Section III on 'Exteriority and the Face' are most relevant for the ethics of witness in trauma research. Levinas argues that the encounter with the Other's face constitutes an irreducible moral demand that precedes all theory — a claim with profound implications for how researchers should relate to trauma survivors.

---

### Zahavi, D. (2019). *Phenomenology: The Basics.* Routledge.

The best accessible introduction to phenomenological philosophy for scholars coming from a psychological rather than philosophical background. Covers Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, and contemporary phenomenology clearly and accurately. Should be read before — or alongside — the primary texts.

---

## II. Phenomenological Psychology and Method

### Giorgi, A. (2009). *The Descriptive Phenomenological Method in Psychology: A Modified Husserlian Approach.* Duquesne University Press.

The definitive methodological text for Giorgi's approach. Chapters 4–7 describe the four-step analysis procedure in detail with worked examples. Essential for any doctoral scholar designing a study using this method. Giorgi's arguments for why phenomenological psychology is genuinely scientific — without being natural-scientific — are also important for methodology chapters.

---

### van Manen, M. (1990). *Researching Lived Experience: Human Science for an Action Sensitive Pedagogy.* SUNY Press.

An accessible and practically oriented account of hermeneutic phenomenological research. The framework of four lifeworld existentials (spatiality, temporality, embodiment, relationality) is directly applicable to research design and data analysis. Chapter 5 on 'Linguistic Transformations' provides useful guidance on phenomenological writing. More interpretive than Giorgi's method; appropriate for studies where the researcher's hermeneutic engagement is foregrounded.

---

### Smith, J. A., Flowers, P., & Larkin, M. (2009). *Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis: Theory, Method, and Research.* SAGE.

The standard reference for IPA. Chapter 2 on epistemology and phenomenological foundations, and Chapter 3 on the analytical process, are essential. Scholars should note that IPA is explicitly interpretive — it draws on phenomenological philosophy but does not claim to produce purely eidetic knowledge. Read alongside primary phenomenological sources to understand what IPA retains and what it modifies from the tradition.

---

### Moustakas, C. (1994). *Phenomenological Research Methods.* SAGE.

Describes the transcendental phenomenological method developed from Husserl, with particular attention to the epoche and horizontalisation. Less rigorous than Giorgi in its philosophical grounding but widely used, particularly in US educational and counselling research. Useful comparison point for understanding different operationalisations of phenomenological method.

---

### Finlay, L. (2011). *Phenomenology for Therapists: Researching the Lived World.* Wiley-Blackwell.

The most accessible bridge between phenomenological research methods and clinical practice. Excellent on reflexivity, the researcher's embodied presence in the research encounter, and the ethics of researching vulnerable populations. Recommended particularly for clinician-researchers who are new to phenomenological methodology.

---

## III. Trauma: Phenomenological and Clinical Perspectives

### Herman, J. L. (1992). *Trauma and Recovery: The Aftermath of Violence — From Domestic Abuse to Political Terror.* Basic Books.

A landmark clinical text whose tripartite model of trauma recovery (safety, remembrance and mourning, reconnection with ordinary life) has deep phenomenological resonances, particularly in its account of intersubjective rupture and repair. Herman's concept of complex trauma and its distinctive clinical presentation has been subsequently validated by the ICD-11 inclusion of Complex PTSD. Essential reading regardless of methodological orientation.

---

### Brison, S. J. (2002). *Aftermath: Violence and the Remaking of a Self.* Princeton University Press.

A philosopher's first-person account of surviving sexual violence, written with phenomenological sensitivity and philosophical rigour. An exemplary integration of lived experience and philosophical reflection — particularly valuable for the analysis of narrative identity disruption, intersubjective collapse, and the role of testimony in recovery. Demonstrates what phenomenological engagement with trauma looks like when done by a philosophical practitioner.

---

### van der Kolk, B. (2014). *The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma.* Viking.

An accessible account of trauma's somatic dimensions that resonates strongly with Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of embodiment. Doctoral scholars should engage critically with its neuroscientific framing from a phenomenological standpoint — asking what the phenomenological account adds to, and how it reframes, the neuroscientific picture. The clinical chapters on body-oriented treatment approaches are directly relevant to the phenomenological account of bodily re-inhabitation.

---

### Stolorow, R. D. (2007). *Trauma and Human Existence: Autobiographical, Psychoanalytic, and Philosophical Reflections.* Routledge.

Brings phenomenological philosophy (particularly Heidegger) into dialogue with trauma theory and psychoanalytic clinical practice. Stolorow's concept of 'emotional dwelling' and his analysis of how trauma disrupts the intersubjective context of emotional experience are particularly valuable. Recommended for scholars interested in integrating phenomenological and psychoanalytic perspectives.

---

## IV. Resilience and Post-Traumatic Growth

### Tedeschi, R. G., & Calhoun, L. G. (2004). Posttraumatic growth: Conceptual foundations and empirical evidence. *Psychological Inquiry, 15*(1), 1–18.

The foundational empirical paper on post-traumatic growth (PTG). Doctoral scholars should read this alongside phenomenological accounts of growth to understand what is and is not captured by the empirical-quantitative framework. The five domains of PTG (personal strength, new possibilities, relating to others, appreciation of life, spiritual/existential change) map interestingly but imperfectly onto phenomenological accounts of lifeworld transformation.

---

### Frankl, V. (1946/1985). *Man's Search for Meaning.* Washington Square Press.

Frankl's logotherapy is phenomenologically informed, and his account of meaning-making in extremis has direct relevance to post-traumatic resilience. To be read as a clinical-philosophical text rather than as memoir alone. The concept of meaning as a fundamental human motivation, and the capacity to find meaning even in conditions of extreme suffering, anticipates aspects of the phenomenological account of resilience developed in the anchor text.

---

### Bonanno, G. A. (2004). Loss, trauma, and human resilience. *American Psychologist, 59*(1), 20–28.

A significant empirical paper challenging the assumption that trauma invariably produces prolonged distress. Bonanno's data on natural resilience trajectories should be read critically from a phenomenological perspective: what does a trajectory of 'minimal disruption' look like from the inside? Does the absence of measured symptoms necessarily correspond to the absence of lifeworld disruption?

---

### Masten, A. S. (2014). *Ordinary Magic: Resilience in Development.* Guilford Press.

A comprehensive developmental account of resilience that situates it in ordinary human adaptive systems rather than extraordinary qualities of exceptional individuals. The concept of 'ordinary magic' — resilience as the expected outcome of healthy development — provides an interesting contrast with the phenomenological account, which emphasises the transformative and often hard-won character of post-traumatic resilience.

---

## V. Ethics and Research with Vulnerable Populations

### Josselson, R. (2007). The ethical attitude in narrative research. In D. J. Clandinin (Ed.), *Handbook of Narrative Inquiry: Mapping a Methodology* (pp. 537–566). SAGE.

Though focused on narrative research, Josselson's account of the ethical attitude — particularly the tension between the researcher's analytic gaze and the participant's vulnerability — is directly applicable to phenomenological research with trauma survivors. Her concept of the 'ethical attitude' as a disposition that precedes and exceeds formal ethics codes resonates with the Levinasian dimension of research ethics.

---

### Guillemin, M., & Gillam, L. (2004). Ethics, reflexivity, and 'ethically important moments' in research. *Qualitative Inquiry, 10*(2), 261–280.

Distinguishes between procedural ethics (ethics committee approval) and ethics in practice — the 'ethically important moments' that arise unexpectedly in the research encounter. The concept of ethically important moments is directly applicable to phenomenological research with trauma survivors, where the depth of the interview method can produce unexpected disclosures and relational demands.

---

*These materials should be used alongside direct engagement with primary phenomenological texts. No secondary account is a substitute for the philosophical originals.*
