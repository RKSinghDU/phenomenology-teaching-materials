# Annotated Bibliography

Texts organised by thematic cluster. Doctoral scholars are expected to engage with primary texts in their full form rather than through secondary summaries.

---

## I. Philosophical Foundations

**Husserl, E. (1913/1982). *Ideas: General Introduction to Pure Phenomenology* (F. Kersten, Trans.). Nijhoff.**

The foundational text of phenomenology. Parts I and II are essential for understanding intentionality, the epoché, and eidetic reduction. Difficult but irreplaceable. Priority sections: §§1–17 (natural attitude), §§27–62 (epoché and reduction), §§86–96 (intentionality).

---

**Heidegger, M. (1927/1962). *Being and Time* (J. Macquarrie & E. Robinson, Trans.). Harper & Row.**

The existential analytic that transformed phenomenology. Division One (§§12–42) on being-in-the-world, spatiality, and mood is directly applicable to trauma phenomenology. Companion: Dreyfus, H. L. (1991). *Being-in-the-World.* MIT Press.

---

**Merleau-Ponty, M. (1945/2012). *Phenomenology of Perception* (D. Landes, Trans.). Routledge.**

Essential for embodiment. Part I (The Body) and Part II (The World as Perceived) are most directly relevant. The Landes translation (2012) is more accurate and readable than the older Smith translation.

---

**Levinas, E. (1969). *Totality and Infinity* (A. Lingis, Trans.). Duquesne University Press.**

Section III on 'Exteriority and the Face' is most relevant for the ethics of witness in trauma research. The encounter with the Other's face constitutes an irreducible moral demand that precedes all theory.

---

**Zahavi, D. (2019). *Phenomenology: The Basics.* Routledge.**

Best accessible introduction for scholars coming from a psychological rather than philosophical background. Should be read before — or alongside — the primary texts.

---

## II. Phenomenological Psychology and Method

**Giorgi, A. (2009). *The Descriptive Phenomenological Method in Psychology.* Duquesne University Press.**

The definitive methodological text. Chapters 4–7 describe the four-step analysis procedure with worked examples. Essential for any doctoral scholar designing a study using this method.

---

**van Manen, M. (1990). *Researching Lived Experience.* SUNY Press.**

Accessible and practically oriented. The framework of four lifeworld existentials is directly applicable to research design. More interpretive than Giorgi; appropriate where the researcher's hermeneutic engagement is foregrounded.

---

**Smith, J. A., Flowers, P., & Larkin, M. (2009). *Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis.* SAGE.**

The standard reference for IPA. Note that IPA is explicitly interpretive — it draws on phenomenological philosophy but does not claim to produce purely eidetic knowledge. Read alongside primary sources to understand what IPA retains and modifies from the tradition.

---

**Finlay, L. (2011). *Phenomenology for Therapists.* Wiley-Blackwell.**

The most accessible bridge between phenomenological research methods and clinical practice. Excellent on reflexivity and the ethics of researching vulnerable populations. Recommended for clinician-researchers new to phenomenological methodology.

---

## III. Trauma: Phenomenological and Clinical Perspectives

**Herman, J. L. (1992). *Trauma and Recovery.* Basic Books.**

A landmark clinical text whose tripartite model of recovery (safety, remembrance and mourning, reconnection) has deep phenomenological resonances. Essential reading regardless of methodological orientation.

---

**Brison, S. J. (2002). *Aftermath: Violence and the Remaking of a Self.* Princeton University Press.**

A philosopher's first-person account of surviving sexual violence. Exemplary integration of lived experience and philosophical reflection — particularly valuable for the analysis of narrative identity disruption and the role of testimony in recovery.

---

**van der Kolk, B. (2014). *The Body Keeps the Score.* Viking.**

Accessible account of trauma's somatic dimensions. Doctoral scholars should engage critically with its neuroscientific framing from a phenomenological standpoint — asking what the phenomenological account adds to and how it reframes the neuroscientific picture.

---

**Stolorow, R. D. (2007). *Trauma and Human Existence.* Routledge.**

Brings Heidegger into dialogue with trauma theory and psychoanalytic clinical practice. Particularly valuable for scholars interested in integrating phenomenological and psychoanalytic perspectives.

---

## IV. Resilience and Post-Traumatic Growth

**Tedeschi, R. G., & Calhoun, L. G. (2004). Posttraumatic growth: Conceptual foundations and empirical evidence. *Psychological Inquiry, 15*(1), 1–18.**

The foundational empirical paper on PTG. Read alongside phenomenological accounts of growth to understand what is and is not captured by the empirical-quantitative framework.

---

**Frankl, V. (1946/1985). *Man's Search for Meaning.* Washington Square Press.**

Frankl's logotherapy is phenomenologically informed. His account of meaning-making in extremis has direct relevance to post-traumatic resilience. To be read as a clinical-philosophical text rather than as memoir alone.

---

**Bonanno, G. A. (2004). Loss, trauma, and human resilience. *American Psychologist, 59*(1), 20–28.**

Significant empirical paper on natural resilience trajectories. Read critically from a phenomenological perspective: what does a trajectory of 'minimal disruption' look like from the inside?

---

## V. Ethics and Research with Vulnerable Populations

**Josselson, R. (2007). The ethical attitude in narrative research. In D. J. Clandinin (Ed.), *Handbook of Narrative Inquiry* (pp. 537–566). SAGE.**

The tension between researcher's analytic gaze and participant vulnerability is directly applicable to phenomenological research with trauma survivors. Her concept of the 'ethical attitude' resonates with the Levinasian dimension of research ethics.

---

**Guillemin, M., & Gillam, L. (2004). Ethics, reflexivity, and 'ethically important moments' in research. *Qualitative Inquiry, 10*(2), 261–280.**

Distinguishes procedural ethics from ethics in practice. 'Ethically important moments' that arise unexpectedly in the research encounter are directly relevant to phenomenological research with trauma survivors.

---

*These materials should be used alongside direct engagement with primary phenomenological texts. No secondary account is a substitute for the philosophical originals.*
