# Activity 10 · Write a Phenomenological Description

**Format:** Individual | **Topic:** Method | **Time:** 30 minutes

---

## Description

Students write a 400-word first-person phenomenological description of a sensory or situational experience, applying the epoché to their own writing. A partner then reads the draft and identifies where description lapses into explanation.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Choose one of the following concrete, bounded experiences to describe:
   - The moment just before falling asleep
   - Eating or drinking something unexpectedly bitter or sour
   - Hearing your own name called in a crowd
   - The first few seconds after waking from a vivid dream
   - Waiting for news you are anxious about
   - Entering a completely unfamiliar room or building

2. Write for 15 minutes in the first person, present tense where possible. The core constraint: **describe, do not explain**. Avoid sentences that begin "I felt X *because*..." or "This reminded me of..." or "I think the reason was..." Stay with what is immediately present to awareness.

3. Review your draft. Mark every sentence that:
   - Explains rather than describes ("I was nervous because...")
   - Interprets rather than attends ("This must have been anxiety...")
   - Generalises away from the particular ("People often feel...")
   - Compares to another experience instead of staying present

4. Revise: convert each marked sentence into a purely descriptive one. Strip the explanation, keep the phenomenon.

5. Read your revised description aloud to a partner. The partner's only job: raise their hand every time description lapses back into explanation. Do not discuss — just note the moments.

---

## Materials

Paper or device for writing; a different colour pen for marking.

---

## Debrief Guidance

This exercise builds the core skill of phenomenological writing: the discipline of staying with what appears rather than explaining it away. It is harder than it sounds — the drive to explain is deeply habitual in academic writing.

Discussion questions:

- Why is pure description so difficult? What does our drive to explain reveal about how we ordinarily orient to experience?

- Connect to van Manen's concept of the **phenomenological nod**: a good phenomenological description should prompt recognition in readers who have had similar experiences — they nod because the description captures something they recognise as true to their own experience. Did your partner's description achieve this?

- What is the difference between *thick description* (rich, specific, concrete) and *thin description* (general, abstract, theoretical)? Phenomenological description should always be thick.

- Ask: when you removed the explanatory sentences, did the description become *more* or *less* vivid? In most cases, pure description is more vivid — because explanation abstracts away from the particular.
