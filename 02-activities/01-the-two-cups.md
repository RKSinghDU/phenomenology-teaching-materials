# Activity 01 · The Two Cups

**Format:** Pairs | **Topic:** Philosophical Foundations | **Time:** 20 minutes

---

## Description

Partners each describe the same physical object — but one describes it as a physicist (measurements, material properties), the other as someone who just received it as a meaningful gift. The pair then compares what each account captures and misses.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Pick any common object on the desk (a cup, pen, phone, bag).
2. **Partner A:** Describe it in purely objective, third-person terms for 2 minutes — dimensions, material, weight, colour wavelength, atomic composition. No reference to use, meaning, or feeling.
3. **Partner B:** Describe what it is *like* to experience this object — its feel, associations, memories it might trigger, what it is *for*, what it means — for 2 minutes.
4. Together, make two lists:
   - What does Partner A's account contain that Partner B's lacks?
   - What does Partner B's account contain that Partner A's lacks?
5. Each pair shares one observation with the room.

---

## Materials

Any common object; no preparation required.

---

## Debrief Guidance

Draw out the distinction between:
- The **Körper** (body as object) — the body/thing as studied by natural science.
- The **Leib** (lived body/thing) — as experienced from within.

Ask the group: which account tells us more about *human experience* of this object? Which is more 'scientific'? Challenge the assumption that objective = complete.

Connect to Husserl's claim that phenomenology returns us to the *things themselves* — not the physicist's reconstruction of the thing, but how it actually shows up in lived experience.

Key discussion point: Partner A's description is perfectly accurate and yet says nothing about the object as it is *lived*. This is the gap phenomenology is designed to address.
