# Activity 01 · The Two Cups

**Format:** Pairs &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Topic:** Philosophical Foundations &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Time:** 20 minutes

---

## Description

Partners each describe the same physical object — one as a physicist (measurements, material), the other as someone who just received it as a meaningful gift. The pair then compares what each account captures and misses.

---

## Step-by-Step

1. Pick any common object on the desk.
2. **Partner A:** Describe it in purely objective, third-person terms for 2 minutes — dimensions, material, weight, colour wavelength. No reference to use, meaning, or feeling.
3. **Partner B:** Describe what it is *like* to experience this object — its feel, associations, memories it might trigger, what it means — for 2 minutes.
4. Together, make two lists: What does A's account contain that B's lacks? What does B's contain that A's lacks?
5. Each pair shares one observation with the room.

---

## Materials

Any common object; no preparation required.

---

## Debrief Guidance

Draw out the distinction between:
- The **Körper** (body/thing as object — studied by natural science)
- The **Leib** (lived body/thing — as experienced from within)

Ask: which account tells us more about *human experience* of this object? Which is more 'scientific'? Challenge the assumption that objective = complete.

Connect to Husserl's claim that phenomenology returns us to the *things themselves* — not the physicist's reconstruction, but how the thing actually shows up in lived experience. Partner A's description is perfectly accurate and yet says nothing about the object as it is *lived*. This is the gap phenomenology is designed to address.
