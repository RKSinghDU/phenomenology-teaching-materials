# Activity 08 · Phenomenological Interview — Live Demonstration

**Format:** Whole class | **Topic:** Method | **Time:** 45 minutes

---

## Description

The instructor interviews a student volunteer about a mundane lived experience in front of the class, modelling phenomenological interview technique. The class observes and then critiques the quality of probes used.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. **Frame the activity** (5 min): The instructor explains that a phenomenological interview seeks rich, first-person description — not explanation, theory, or interpretation. The interviewer follows the participant's own meaning rather than imposing a predetermined structure.

2. **Opening question** (use one of the following):
   - *"Tell me about a time you experienced waiting — describe it as concretely and fully as you can."*
   - *"Describe for me what it is like to wake up in the morning — from the very first moment of awareness."*
   - *"Tell me about an experience of feeling completely lost — in any sense of that word."*

3. **Interview** (15–20 min): Instructor conducts the interview using only phenomenologically sensitive probes. The class observes silently.

4. **Class observation task**: While watching, note on paper:
   - Which probes *opened* description (participant gave richer, more detailed responses)?
   - Which probes *narrowed* description (participant became more general, defensive, or explanatory)?
   - Were there moments the interviewer moved toward explanation rather than description?

5. **Critique** (15 min): Class shares observations. Instructor uses these to draw out principles of phenomenological interviewing.

---

## Phenomenologically Sensitive Probes

Use *only* these types of probes during the demonstration:

| Probe Type | Example |
|------------|---------|
| **Opening** | "Can you tell me more about that?" |
| **Descriptive deepening** | "What was that like for you?" |
| **Bodily attention** | "What were you aware of in your body at that moment?" |
| **Temporal** | "What happened just before that? And then what?" |
| **Spatial** | "Where were you? What was the space like?" |
| **Relational** | "Was anyone else there? How did their presence feel?" |

**Probes to avoid** (explain why during debrief):

| Probe to Avoid | Why It's Problematic |
|----------------|----------------------|
| "Why do you think you felt that?" | Shifts from description to explanation |
| "Did you feel anxious?" | Imposes category; closes description |
| "That sounds like it was stressful" | Reflects and validates but doesn't deepen description |
| "Have you felt this before?" | Comparative; pulls away from the present experience |

---

## Materials

Open space; two chairs facing each other at the front. No notes or devices for the interviewer.

---

## Debrief Guidance

The central learning point: a phenomenological interview is not a conversation, a therapy session, or a structured interview. It is disciplined, sustained, attentive *following* — the interviewer stays in the participant's world rather than pulling them into the interviewer's categories.

Ask the class to compare: how would this interview look if conducted by:
- A cognitive psychologist? (Would likely ask about thoughts, appraisals, beliefs)
- A journalist? (Would seek narrative arc, causation, significance)
- A doctor taking a history? (Would seek symptoms, timeline, functional impact)

Each approach reveals something; each also conceals something. What does the phenomenological approach specifically reveal that the others cannot? What does it conceal?

This question directly demonstrates why methodological choice is not neutral — it shapes what can and cannot appear as data.
