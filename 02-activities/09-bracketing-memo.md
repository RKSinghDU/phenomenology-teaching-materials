# Activity 09 · Bracketing Memo — Researcher Reflexivity

**Format:** Individual | **Topic:** Method | **Time:** 20 minutes

---

## Description

Students write a structured reflexive memo documenting their prior assumptions, theoretical commitments, and personal connections to a given research topic — then identify specifically what would need to be bracketed before beginning phenomenological analysis.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. The instructor announces a research topic (e.g., *resilience in family caregivers*, *the experience of chronic pain*, *academic failure in first-generation university students*).
2. Students write freely for 12 minutes, responding to these four prompts:
   - **What do I already believe about this topic?** (Assumptions, convictions, hunches)
   - **Which theories or frameworks shape how I think about it?** (CBT, attachment theory, biomedical model, etc.)
   - **What personal experience do I bring?** (Direct experience, vicarious, professional)
   - **What do I expect to find if I research this?** (Anticipated themes, likely findings)
3. Review what you have written. Using a different colour or underlining, mark every statement that could *bias your reading* of participant data — assumptions you might unconsciously confirm, categories you might impose, interpretations you might reach too quickly.
4. Identify 3 specific things you would need to actively bracket before and during analysis. Write them as explicit statements: *"I will need to bracket my assumption that..."*
5. Exchange your memo with a partner. Each partner identifies one additional blind spot the writer did not notice.

---

## Materials

Paper or device for writing; different colour pen for marking (optional).

---

## Debrief Guidance

Bracketing is not forgetting — it is the disciplined *acknowledgement* of what you bring, so that you can set it temporarily aside while analysing data. You cannot bracket what you have not first made explicit.

Key questions for group discussion:

**Can bracketing ever be complete?** No — this is one of the honest limitations of phenomenological method. The researcher can never achieve a view from nowhere. The goal is not zero-assumption research but *minimally assumption-laden, maximally description-responsive* research.

**How does phenomenological bracketing compare to reflexivity in other qualitative traditions?**
- In grounded theory: reflexivity involves acknowledging positionality but does not require systematic suspension of prior categories.
- In feminist research: the researcher's standpoint is a resource, not something to be bracketed.
- In phenomenological research: the epoch takes a more radical stance — prior frameworks are explicitly set aside *for the duration of analysis*, then re-engaged at the interpretive stage.

**Why does this matter for trauma research specifically?** Researchers who have personal trauma histories bring both insight and risk: the risk of over-identification (reading their own experience into the data), under-identification (defending against data that is too close), or secondary traumatisation. The bracketing memo is one element of an adequate ethical-methodological response to this.
