# Activity 12 · Ethics of Witness — Case Deliberation

**Format:** Small group &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Topic:** Ethics &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Time:** 40 minutes

---

## Description

Groups receive a brief case scenario describing a researcher who encounters unexpectedly traumatic material during an interview. They deliberate on the ethical obligations entailed by the phenomenological stance of witness — and draft a response policy.

---

## Case Scenario

> Dr. Meera is conducting phenomenological interviews with adults about their experience of returning to education after a long break. Fifteen minutes into an interview, the participant begins to describe how their withdrawal from education was linked to a period of severe domestic violence. They become visibly distressed and describe experiences of fear and helplessness in considerable detail. Dr. Meera had not prepared a trauma referral protocol, had not flagged the possibility of traumatic disclosure in the participant information sheet, and is herself a survivor of a difficult relationship.

---

## Deliberation Questions

1. What does Dr. Meera owe this participant *as a witness* in this moment?
2. What obligations arise from having *invited* this testimony through the interview?
3. What does a **Levinasian ethics of the Other** demand here — beyond standard research ethics codes?
4. How does Dr. Meera's own history as a survivor affect her ethical obligations? Is it an asset, a liability, or both?
5. What should have been done *before* the interview to prepare for this possibility?

---

## Step-by-Step

1. Read the scenario together (3 min).
2. Deliberate on the questions above (20 min).
3. Draft a one-paragraph **ethical response policy** for phenomenological research with general populations that may inadvertently disclose traumatic material.
4. Present your policy to the room. Compare across groups — where do policies converge and diverge?

---

## Materials

Printed case scenario; paper or device for drafting the policy.

---

## Debrief Guidance

Draw on Levinas's claim that the **Other's face** makes an irreducible moral demand — a demand that precedes and exceeds any formal ethics code.

Key tensions to surface:

- **Autonomy vs. welfare:** Standard ethics emphasises participant autonomy; welfare considerations may require the researcher to redirect or close down disclosure if the participant appears in acute distress.
- **Researcher competence:** A phenomenological researcher is not a therapist. What are the limits of researcher role, and how should those limits be communicated in advance?
- **The researcher's own history:** Personal trauma history brings both insight and risk. The bracketing memo, supervision, and personal therapy are ethical necessities, not optional extras.

A well-designed protocol addressing vulnerable populations should include: trauma-informed screening, honest participant information, referral pathways prepared in advance, researcher supervision built into the study design, and a reflexive plan for managing researcher distress.
