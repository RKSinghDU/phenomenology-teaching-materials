# Activity 12 · Ethics of Witness — Case Deliberation

**Format:** Small group | **Topic:** Ethics | **Time:** 40 minutes

---

## Description

Groups receive a brief case scenario describing a researcher who encounters unexpectedly traumatic material during an interview. They deliberate on the ethical obligations entailed by the phenomenological stance of witness — and draft a response policy grounded in both phenomenological ethics and research ethics principles.

---

## Case Scenario

*(Instructor may adapt this scenario to fit their disciplinary context.)*

> Dr. Meera is conducting phenomenological interviews with adults about their experience of returning to education after a long break. Fifteen minutes into an interview, the participant — who gave no indication of a difficult history during screening — begins to describe how their withdrawal from education was linked to a period of severe domestic violence. They become visibly distressed, tearful, and start to describe experiences of fear and helplessness in considerable detail. Dr. Meera had not prepared a trauma referral protocol, had not flagged the possibility of traumatic disclosure in the participant information sheet, and is herself a survivor of a difficult relationship.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Read the scenario together (3 min).

2. Deliberate on the following questions (20 min):
   - What does Dr. Meera owe this participant *as a witness* in this moment? What should she do immediately?
   - What obligations arise from having *invited* this testimony through the interview?
   - What does a **Levinasian ethics of the Other** demand in this situation — beyond standard research ethics codes?
   - How does Dr. Meera's own history as a survivor affect her ethical obligations? Is it an asset, a liability, or both?
   - What should have been done *before* the interview to prepare for this possibility?

3. Draft a one-paragraph **ethical response policy** for phenomenological research with general populations that may inadvertently disclose traumatic material. This policy should be usable in an ethics application.

4. Present your policy to the room. Compare across groups — where do policies converge and diverge?

---

## Materials

Printed case scenario; paper or device for drafting the policy.

---

## Debrief Guidance

Draw on Levinas's claim that the **Other's face** makes an irreducible moral demand — a demand that precedes and exceeds any formal ethical code. The phenomenological researcher is not a neutral recorder; they have entered into a relationship that carries obligations the moment the participant begins to speak.

Key tensions to surface in discussion:

**Autonomy vs. welfare:** Standard research ethics emphasises participant autonomy — the right to disclose what they choose, to have that disclosure received and recorded. But welfare considerations may require the researcher to interrupt, redirect, or close down disclosure if the participant appears to be in distress. How are these balanced?

**Researcher competence:** A phenomenological researcher is not a therapist. They are not trained to manage acute traumatic disclosure. What are the limits of researcher role, and how should those limits be communicated to participants in advance?

**The researcher's own history:** Phenomenological method acknowledges that researchers bring their whole selves to the encounter. A researcher with personal trauma history brings insight *and* risk. The bracketing memo (Activity 09) is one response; supervision and personal therapy are others. What ethical structures are required?

**What standard research ethics frameworks (autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice) capture — and what they miss:**
- They capture the obligation to avoid harm and to respect autonomy.
- They miss the Levinasian dimension: the asymmetrical, irreducible responsibility that arises from the encounter with the Other's vulnerability — a responsibility that cannot be fully codified in advance.

A well-designed phenomenological research protocol addressing vulnerable populations should include: trauma-informed screening, clear and honest participant information about the possibility of emotional distress, referral pathways prepared in advance, researcher supervision built into the study design, and a reflexive plan for managing researcher distress.
