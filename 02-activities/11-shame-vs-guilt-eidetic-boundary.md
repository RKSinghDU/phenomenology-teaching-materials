# Activity 11 · Shame vs. Guilt — Eidetic Boundary

**Format:** Small group | **Topic:** Philosophical Foundations | **Time:** 35 minutes

---

## Description

Groups work to establish an eidetic boundary between shame and guilt — two phenomena often conflated in clinical literature but phenomenologically distinct. They use imaginative variation to isolate the essential structure of each and then present their definitions for whole-class critique.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Each group generates 4 concrete example situations — 2 clearly involving *shame*, 2 clearly involving *guilt*. Make examples specific and vivid.

2. Apply imaginative variation to key features of each phenomenon:

   **For shame — test these features:**
   - Remove the social witness (real or imagined). Does shame remain shame if no one — not even an imagined observer — is present?
   - Remove the action. Can you feel shame about *what you are* rather than *what you did*?
   - Remove the self-focus. Can shame be primarily about an external event rather than about the self?

   **For guilt — test these features:**
   - Remove the specific action. Can you feel guilt about *who you are* rather than *what you did*?
   - Remove awareness of harm to another. Can guilt exist without a sense of having wronged someone?
   - Remove the possibility of repair or making amends. Does guilt lose its character without the pull toward reparation?

3. Formulate one-sentence eidetic definitions of each:
   - *"Shame is essentially..."*
   - *"Guilt is essentially..."*

4. Present definitions to the whole class for critique. Other groups identify counter-examples that challenge the proposed definitions.

---

## Materials

Whiteboard or large paper per group.

---

## Debrief Guidance

The clinical and psychological literature converges on an important distinction:

- **Shame:** About exposure of the *self* — "I am bad / defective / worthless." Shame tends to require a (real or imagined) social witness — someone before whom one is exposed. It is global, self-referential, and tends to produce hiding, withdrawal, or rage.

- **Guilt:** About evaluation of a specific *action* — "I did something wrong." Guilt does not require an audience. It is specific, action-referential, and tends to produce reparative motivation — apology, making amends, behavioural change.

**Why this matters clinically and for trauma research:**

Ask why shame is so prominent in interpersonal trauma — particularly sexual violence, childhood abuse, and torture. The phenomenological answer: these forms of trauma are designed to constitute the *self* as exposed, contaminated, and defective — not merely to make the survivor feel bad about something they did. The eidetic distinction between shame and guilt explains why treatment approaches that focus on cognitive reappraisal of specific events may be insufficient for shame-based presentations; the target of intervention is the self-structure, not a specific belief about a specific action.

Connect to the work of June Price Tangney and Thomas Scheff on the distinction between shame and guilt, and to the clinical literature on shame in Complex PTSD.
