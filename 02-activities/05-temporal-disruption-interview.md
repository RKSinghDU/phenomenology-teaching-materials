# Activity 05 · Temporal Disruption Interview

**Format:** Pairs | **Topic:** Trauma & Resilience | **Time:** 35 minutes

---

## Description

Using a published literary account of time-distortion (provided by the instructor), pairs analyse how lived time differs from clock time — identifying the specific temporal features present in the text and mapping them onto Husserl's structure of time-consciousness.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Read a 200-word excerpt describing distorted time experience (instructor provides — suggested: a passage from a survivor memoir or literary fiction describing dissociation or flashback, used with appropriate permissions).
2. **Partner A:** Map every temporal feature in the excerpt onto a three-column grid:
   - Past (retentions, memories, intrusions)
   - Present (current moment, bodily awareness)
   - Future (anticipation, projection, foreclosure)
3. **Partner B:** Note every place where *clock time* and *lived time* diverge — where the subjective temporal experience contradicts or exceeds the objective timeline.
4. Together: write 3 sentences describing the essential structure of temporal disruption as visible in this account.
5. Share with another pair and compare your structural descriptions.

## Debrief Guidance

Introduce Husserl's retention/protention structure of time-consciousness:
- **Retentions:** The just-passed moment still held in living awareness.
- **Protentions:** The about-to-come moment already anticipated.
- **The living present:** Not a knife-edge instant but a thick, flowing 'now' containing both.

In trauma, this structure is catastrophically disrupted:
- The traumatic past is not retained as *past* but re-experienced as *present* (flashback as temporal invasion).
- Protentions toward the future are blocked — the future feels absent or threatening rather than open.
- The 'living present' is colonised by an event that refuses to become fully historical.

Ask: can you imagine a phenomenology of traumatic memory that is *not* also a phenomenology of time? What does this suggest about why purely content-focused trauma therapies (simply 'processing the memory') may be insufficient if they do not address the temporal structure of the experience?
