# Activity 06 · Intersubjectivity Collapse — The Fishbowl

**Format:** Whole class | **Topic:** Trauma & Resilience | **Time:** 40 minutes

---

## Description

Three volunteers sit in an inner circle and attempt to describe an experience that was difficult to communicate to people who were not there. The outer circle observes the linguistic and relational dynamics without intervening — attending to where language strains, hesitates, or reaches for metaphor.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Instructor frames the activity (3 min): trauma creates an intersubjective rupture — the event resists ordinary language and shared understanding. This activity explores the experience of that rupture using non-traumatic examples.
2. Arrange the room: 3 chairs in the centre (inner circle), remaining students around the outside (outer circle).
3. **Inner circle** (3 volunteers): each describes a non-traumatic experience that was nonetheless genuinely difficult to communicate — something others 'couldn't quite understand unless they were there.' Speak directly to each other, not to the audience. (10–12 min)
4. **Outer circle**: observe silently. Note:
   - Where does language seem to strain or fall short?
   - Where does the speaker reach for metaphor, gesture, or pause?
   - Where does the listener seem to *almost* understand — and where does understanding visibly fail?
5. Switch inner/outer roles after 15 minutes if time allows.
6. Whole-class debrief (15 min).

---

## Materials

Room arrangement with inner/outer circles; chairs only.

---

## Safety Note

Volunteers should be told in advance that this exercise is about non-traumatic experiences of communicative difficulty — not about sharing painful or distressing material. Give a clear example: arriving at a university from a very different background; witnessing a strange event abroad; a moment of unexpected beauty no photograph could capture.

---

## Debrief Guidance

Draw out Levinas's concept of **alterity** — the irreducible otherness of the Other. Even in ordinary experience, perfect mutual understanding is impossible; the Other's experience is never fully transparent to me.

Connect to Judith Herman's observation that trauma creates a **double bind of testimony**:
- The experience is *unspeakable* — too extreme for ordinary language, too discontinuous with shared reality.
- The experience is simultaneously *urgent to be heard* — the survivor needs witness.

This double bind — needing to be heard while being unable to fully communicate — is itself part of the phenomenological structure of trauma.

Ask the class: what does *good witnessing* require? This is not primarily a technical skill (active listening techniques) but an existential stance — a willingness to remain present with what cannot be fully understood. How does this translate into the therapeutic relationship? Into the role of the phenomenological researcher?
