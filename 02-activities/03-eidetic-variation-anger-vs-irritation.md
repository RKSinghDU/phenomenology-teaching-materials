# Activity 03 · Eidetic Variation: Anger vs. Irritation

**Format:** Small group | **Topic:** Philosophical Foundations | **Time:** 30 minutes

---

## Description

Groups apply free imaginative variation to identify what is essential to anger versus what is essential to irritation — attempting to draw an eidetic boundary between two closely related but phenomenologically distinct phenomena.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Assign each group one phenomenon: either *anger* or *irritation*.
2. Generate 5 vivid, concrete example instances of your phenomenon — vary intensity, context, target, duration, and social setting.
3. Apply imaginative variation to each key feature:
   - Remove the feature imaginatively. Does the phenomenon remain the same kind?
   - If yes → the feature is **non-essential** (accidental to this instance).
   - If no → the feature is **essential** (eidetically constitutive of the kind).
4. Compile two lists: essential features and non-essential features.
5. Groups present their eidetic structures to each other and compare — where do anger and irritation diverge? Where do they overlap?

---

## Materials

Whiteboard or large sticky notes per group.

---

## Debrief Guidance

Highlight where groups disagree — this is philosophically productive, not a failure. Disagreements reveal where further imaginative variation is needed.

Common findings to draw out:
- **Duration:** Irritation tends to be briefer and more diffuse; anger can be sustained and focused.
- **Target:** Anger tends to be more clearly directed toward a specific agent or action perceived as wrong; irritation can be objectless or directed at circumstances.
- **Moral dimension:** Anger often carries a sense of wrongness or injustice; irritation does not necessarily.
- **Intensity:** Anger typically involves higher arousal and motivational urgency.

Connect to Husserl's claim: eidetic knowledge is *a priori* — we do not need a survey of thousands of angry people to know what is essential to anger. We need only sufficiently varied imaginative examples and rigorous reflection.

Ask: what does this exercise tell us about how phenomenological research differs from inductive empirical generalisation?
