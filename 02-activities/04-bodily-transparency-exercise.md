# Activity 04 · Bodily Transparency Exercise

**Format:** Individual | **Topic:** Philosophical Foundations | **Time:** 10 minutes

---

## Description

Students write about a skilled activity they perform without conscious attention to their body — then attempt to perform it while deliberately attending to every movement. Comparing the two experiences reveals Merleau-Ponty's concept of bodily transparency.

---

## Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Write for 3 minutes: describe a skilled activity you perform without thinking — typing, driving, playing an instrument, a sport, cooking a familiar dish. What do you attend to *while* doing it? (Usually: the task, not the body performing it.)
2. Now perform the activity — or mime it physically if not possible in the room — while consciously attending to every bodily movement: each finger placement, each shift of weight, each breath.
3. Write for 2 minutes: what changes? What becomes awkward, slow, or unfamiliar?
4. Share one sentence describing the difference with the group.

---

## Materials

None required.

---

## Debrief Guidance

This exercise demonstrates Merleau-Ponty's concept of **bodily transparency**: in skilled action, the lived body normally 'disappears' into our projects. We do not perceive our fingers when we type — we perceive the words appearing. The body is the *medium*, not the object, of skilled experience.

When deliberate attention is directed at the body, performance typically degrades — the phenomenon known as 'paralysis by analysis.' This is not a quirk of psychology; it reveals the pre-reflective, transparent character of the lived body in ordinary experience.

**Connection to trauma:** Ask — when does your body *not* disappear? In pain, illness, embarrassment, and threat, the body suddenly becomes conspicuous and obtrusive. In trauma, this rupture of transparency becomes chronic: the body is perpetually hyper-present, a site of alarm rather than a transparent vehicle of engagement.

Ask: what does this suggest about why body-oriented therapies (somatic experiencing, EMDR, yoga) might be particularly relevant to trauma treatment? If trauma is stored in the pre-reflective body, can purely verbal, cognitive therapy fully reach it?
